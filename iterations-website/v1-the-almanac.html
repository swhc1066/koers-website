Mia is built from sessions written by real coaches — Joe Friel's periodization, Stephen Seiler's polarized work, the gravel-specific intensity distributions our coaching panel uses with their athletes. The plan adapts when sleep, HRV, life, or the weather change. You ride; Mia rewrites.

For riders with a human coach, Mia steps aside. Koers becomes the journal — your coach writes the plan, Mia writes the kickers.

III.

A month with Koers, in three chapters.

You'll be on your bike Tuesday. The whole signup is a short conversation, an FTP guess, and one A-race on the calendar.
I.

Tell Mia about you.

A six-minute conversation, no surveys with sliders. Where you live, when you ride, what you're aiming at, how you felt last season. Bring an FTP if you have one, a guess if you don't.

Day one · Sunday"You said you want to finish Unbound in under 14 hours. Let's work backwards from June 1st."
II.

Mia writes the first month.

Five sessions a week, three of them under an hour. The first week is calibration — Mia is measuring you. By week two the workouts get specific, with a real why beside every interval.

Day two · Monday"Tomorrow is short. I want to see what your legs feel like before I trust the FTP you sent me."
III.

The plan responds to the body.

You ride; the file comes in from Garmin or Wahoo; Mia adjusts. Sometimes that means a harder Friday, more often a softer Wednesday. You see the change as a tracked edit, with a one-sentence reason.

Day fifteen · Monday"Wednesday's intervals moved to Friday. Your HRV is low and the forecast is rain. Don't argue with it."
IV.

Why Koers is built on fatigue, not streaks.

An essay in two columns. A short note from Mia on the method, with a footer of the sources we read.
An essay

The training apps you've used measure the wrong thing. They count rides. They reward the streak. They congratulate you on the easy Saturday spin and stay quiet on the day you should have rested.

Koers measures fatigue. Specifically: Training Stress Balance — the rolling difference between the fitness you've built over the last six weeks and the fatigue you've accumulated in the last seven days. It's the same number a WorldTour coach uses to taper a rider before the Tour. It's also a number you can ignore most of the time, because Mia reads it for you.

What you'll see, instead, is the sentence. "You're at −4 TSB, exactly where I want you before Unbound." Or: "We've been at +8 for two weeks. Tomorrow needs to hurt." The number is under the hood. The decision is on the page.

This is the difference between a training tool and a coach: a tool shows you the dashboard. A coach reads the dashboard and tells you one sentence.
